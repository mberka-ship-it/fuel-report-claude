Forward Analysis — What Vessel Traffic Won't Show You Yet
Structural & pipeline signals · ASPI · CSIS · IEA OMR March 2026 · Macquarie Business School · Al Jazeera · CNBC · Atlantic Council
Why this section exists: Tanker arrivals at Brisbane tell you what has already happened. The signals below are upstream structural changes — in refineries, insurance markets, crude grades, and geography — that will determine what arrives in April and May. None of them are yet visible in vessel tracking data.
① Crude Grade Mismatch — The Hidden Refinery Problem
Asian refineries — including the Korean and Singaporean plants that supply 45% of Australia's fuel — are physically configured for medium and heavy sour crude from the Gulf. The IEA's March 2026 Oil Market Report notes Saudi Arabia is cutting production, and the missing barrels are mostly Medium and Heavy grades that cannot easily be substituted. Replacement barrels from the US or West Africa are typically light and sweet. Switching crude slates requires technical reconfiguration and blending that reduces refinery efficiency and can create shortfalls in specific distillates — particularly diesel and jet fuel — even when replacement volume is nominally available.

Sources: IEA OMR March 2026 · Atlantic Council via statt.com
② Gulf Refineries Filling Up — Export-Oriented Plants Cutting Runs
With Hormuz closed and tankers unable to load, Gulf export-oriented refineries are cutting run rates because storage tanks are filling up with nowhere to send product. The IEA estimates more than 4 mb/d of refining capacity is at risk of curtailment. Gulf producers exported 3.3 mb/d of refined products and 1.5 mb/d of LPG in 2025 — that flow has largely ceased. This reduction is not yet visible in Australian port arrivals because tankers en route before the closure are still arriving. The pipeline drains in approximately 3–4 weeks from the date of closure — pointing to mid-to-late April as the moment when reduced loadings begin materialising as reduced arrivals at Brisbane.

Source: IEA Oil Market Report, March 2026
③ Australia's Second Vulnerability Layer — Indonesian Straits
ASPI's Strategist (10 Mar) identifies a risk entirely invisible in current vessel data: roughly 83% of Australia's maritime imports and 90% of exports pass through Indonesian straits (Malacca, Lombok, Sunda) before reaching Australian ports. Hormuz is the first vulnerability layer. If Middle Eastern crude disruption also triggers maritime coercion or conflict in Southeast Asian sea lanes — through regional escalation or opportunistic action — Australia would face physically constrained access to refined products with no domestic refining alternative. The ASPI analysis concludes the "centre of gravity in Australia's fuel security debate must shift north" — toward northern Australian geography and internal fuel logistics.

Source: ASPI Strategist, 10 Mar 2026
④ War Risk Insurance Pullout — Structural, Not Temporary
Protection and Indemnity (P&I) war risk insurance for the Strait of Hormuz was withdrawn almost immediately after 28 Feb, making the economic risk prohibitive for ship owners regardless of Iran's selective passage signals. The Wikipedia crisis log notes war risk cover was removed for 5 March transit, and Oman's alternative ports (Duqm, Salalah, Sohar) are now also in insurers' war risk zones following drone strikes on Duqm and Salalah in March. This matters for Australia because even if Iran selectively allows non-Western vessels through, insurance withdrawal keeps the majority of the commercial tanker fleet — including those carrying product to Australia — unable or unwilling to route via the Gulf. Insurance normalisation typically lags conflict resolution by weeks to months.

Sources: Wikipedia: 2026 Hormuz Crisis · Geopolitical Monitor
⑤ Strategic Reserves Cannot Replace Physical Flow
The IEA's 400M barrel emergency release is the largest ever, but Al Jazeera's analysis (15 Mar) quantifies its limits precisely: at 105 mb/d global consumption, 400M barrels covers just 4 days of global demand, or about 20 days of normal Hormuz flow. CSIS economist Clayton Seigle told CNN: the US Energy Independence offset is "a very small potential offset compared to concerns for the physical supply of refined products." Australia's own MSO release of 762ML covers approximately 3–4 days of national consumption. The release buys time and moderates panic, but cannot substitute for a functioning import pipeline. The key metric to watch is not whether reserves are being released, but whether the rate of depletion of national stocks is slowing or accelerating.

Sources: Al Jazeera, 15 Mar · CNN, 12 Mar
⑥ Diesel & Jet Fuel Most Vulnerable — And Australian Mining at Risk
The IEA's March OMR specifically flags diesel and jet fuel as "particularly vulnerable" due to limited flexibility in non-Gulf regions to increase output. Singapore gasoil (diesel proxy) had already reached $143.88/bbl by 13 March — up 57% from pre-crisis; jet fuel reached $199.66/bbl, +114%. For Australia specifically, a downstream analysis (discoveryalert.com.au) notes Australia's mining sector consumes approximately 40% of national diesel supply — creating a direct policy dilemma: preserve diesel for food distribution and emergency services, or maintain export-dependent mining operations that underpin the national economy. Dr Lurion De Mello (Macquarie Business School) adds that Brisbane's Ampol Lytton refinery exports its high-sulphur output rather than supplying locally, a structural gap that emergency directives are only partially addressing.

Sources: IEA OMR March 2026 · Macquarie Business School, 13 Mar
⑦ NEW TODAY (20 Mar) — Lytton Maintenance Deferred: +300ML Domestic Supply
Ampol announced this morning (ASX) that the scheduled Turnaround and Inspection (T&I) shutdown at the Lytton refinery — originally planned for June 2026 — has been deferred to August 2026. This keeps the refinery running through the peak risk window and delivers approximately 300 million litres of additional petrol, diesel and jet fuel to the domestic market. The FSSP support collar was also raised from 6.4 to 10.0 Acpl, improving Lytton's financial viability during periods of weak margins. Combined with the temporary fuel standard relaxation (adding 80–100ML/month), this is the most significant positive domestic supply signal since the crisis began. It does not replace imports — Australia still needs tankers — but it meaningfully extends the runway before rationing would become necessary.

Sources: Sharecafe — ASX announcement, 20 Mar 2026 · TipRanks · Motley Fool AU
Bonus signal — Fertiliser cascade into food prices (not fuel, but related)
A signal most observers are missing: CSIS analysis confirms nearly half of all seaborne urea exports and 44% of global sulphur production transit Hormuz — far exceeding its oil role as a chokepoint. Urea prices jumped 32% in the first week of the crisis (New Orleans hub: $516 → $683/tonne). Australia's autumn planting window is now. If fertiliser supply is disrupted for the current planting cycle, downstream food price inflation feeds through in Q3–Q4 2026 regardless of when Hormuz reopens. This is a second-order impact on household cost-of-living that won't appear in any fuel price tracker.

Sources: CSIS: Chokepoint — War with Iran and Global Food Security · Food Ingredients First, 16 Mar
